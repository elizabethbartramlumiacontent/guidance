S011C: Supplementary winter bird feeding

Increase bird populations by spreading seed for them during winter and spring when their natural food is harder to find.

Published 17 June 2020
From: Natural England

Why farmland birds need extra food in winter

Farmland bird species have declined since the 1970s. This is partly due to changing farming methods which has decreased the availability of seeds in winter and early spring.

Spreading seed on your land is a simple and effective way to increase the availability of food for farmland birds.

When to start spreading seed

Start spreading seed when other seed sources on your farm start to become depleted. The time this happens varies and depends on:

  • the amount of natural and sown seed available in the autumn
  • the number of birds
  • how harsh the weather is

As a guide, feed from December through to April.

You should start feeding from the beginning of November if your winter bird food plots have failed or it is the first winter of an autumn-sown winter bird food plot. This will replace these plots as a food source.

Choosing appropriate feeding sites

You need to spread seed at more than one site to increase availability and provide seed for species which prefer to feed in different locations. For example, tree sparrows and yellowhammers like to have nearby cover, such as a hedge. Corn buntings prefer sites next to fences or ditches and with vantage points to perch and scan for predators.

Top up the seed at each site on different days to make sure there is always some seed available. You will also reduce the risk of attracting pests, such as rats.

Spread seed on firm, free-draining areas of bare ground or short vegetation to make it easy for birds to find. If you cannot easily see the seeds then the vegetation is too long.

Suitable areas include:

  • winter food bird plots
  • overwinter stubble
  • cultivated fallow areas
  • bare or sparsely vegetated farm tracks in quiet areas
  • areas of hard standing away from farmyards, such as a beet pad
  • regularly mown grass margins with short vegetation

The recommended seed mix

A good general seed mix appropriate for a range of farmland birds:

Seed Percentage
Wheat 50%
Naked oats 20%
Oilseed rape 10%
White millet 10%
Red millet 5%
Canary seed or sunflower hearts 5%

If you farm organically, or are converting to organic farming, you must either use organic seed or get approval from your organic certification body to use non-organic seed.

Adapting the seed mix

Different species prefer feeding on different seeds, depending on the beak size and the seed's nutritional value. You can adapt the recommended seed mix to provide food for certain species if you know they are in your area.

For example, larger buntings such as corn buntings and yellowhammers prefer cereals. So you could add barley or triticale to the recommended seed mix.

Finches prefer smaller, oily seeds. You could increase the proportions of these by adding some linseed or mustard seed.

Some species have seen a greater decline in populations than others so it is especially worthwhile adapting your seed mix to suit these.

For example:

  • corn bunting prefer mostly wheat with smaller amounts of naked oats and barley
  • for tree sparrows increase the amount of red millet and sunflower hearts
  • for grey partridge increase the proportion of cereals in your mix

When adapting the seed mix make sure that:

  • you include at least 2 cereals and 3 small-seeded crops
  • small-seeded crops make up at least 30% of the mix by weight
  • you do not use maize or tic beans as they attract pests and birds prefer other seeds

The Big Farmland Bird Count from the Game and Wildlife Conservation Trust and the Bird Identifier from the Royal Society for the Protection of Birds can help you recognise what is living in your area.

How much to spread

You should always have food available at your feeding sites.

To start with, spread around 25kg of seed each week at every site. Check regularly and adjust how often you feed or the amount you spread to match that needed by the birds. Each feeding situation will be different, so you should check feeding sites regularly and replace seed when most of it has been eaten.

Birds need more seed during periods of cold weather, particular if they can't feed from other sources due to frost or snow.

There should not be large amounts of seed left uneaten. If this happens, lower the amount of seed to reduce waste and the likelihood of the seed being eaten by deer, rats and pigeons.

How to spread seed effectively

Aim for an even spread of seed either by hand or using equipment, such as a clean slug pellet spreader or purpose-built spreader.

To discourage pests like rats and reduce the risk of disease at your feeding sites you should:

  • spin or scatter seed to avoid creating piles of seed or long trails
  • move feeding sites every year to reduce pathogen build-up
  • stop feeding for at least a week if there is a build-up of uneaten food
  • move feeding sites during the same winter, but no more than 50m at a time
